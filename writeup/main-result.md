# 2-group Belyi maps cannot produce a nonsolvable number field ramified only at 2

**Status:** complete except for one geometric input (Lemma B, indecomposability), which is
supported by exhaustive computation (2008/2008 cases through order 128) but not yet proven.
The algebraic core (Lemma A) is formally verified in Lean 4 + Mathlib.

This document consolidates a negative resolution of the "long-term application" of Musty's
2019 Dartmouth thesis *2-Group Belyi Maps* (advisor J. Voight): specifically of the open
questions Q1.2.5 (is `Q(J[2^n])` solvable?) and Q6.2.1 (are the moduli fields solvable?),
for the field generated by the 2-power torsion.

---

## 1. Setup and the target

A **2-group Belyi map** is a Galois cover `φ: X → P¹` branched only over `{0, 1, ∞}` whose
monodromy (deck) group `G` is a finite 2-group. Equivalently it is a surjection from the
free group `F₂ = π₁(P¹ ∖ {0,1,∞})` onto a finite 2-group `G`, i.e. a normal subgroup
`N ◁ F₂` of 2-power index with `F₂/N ≅ G`.

By **Beckmann's theorem**, such an `X` has good reduction away from 2, so for every `n` the
2-power torsion field `Q(J[2^n])` of the Jacobian `J = Jac(X)` is **ramified only at 2**.
The mod-2 Galois representation lands in `Sp_{2g}(F₂)` (`g = genus X`), which for `g ≥ 2` is
nonsolvable (it contains simple composition factors). The thesis's hope: if the arithmetic
image were *large* (nonsolvable), `Q(J[2])` would be an **explicit** nonsolvable number
field ramified only at 2 — obtained cheaply from a computable curve.

Such fields **do exist**: Dembélé (2009) constructed one (nonsolvable, ramified at 2 only)
from a Hilbert modular form over `Q(ζ₃₂)⁺`. But that field is not a small, writable object.
The question here is whether the **2-group Belyi construction** can reach one.

**Answer (this work): No.** For every 2-group Belyi map, `Q(J[2])` — indeed the full
`Q(J[2^∞])` — is solvable. Both the Galois and non-Galois versions of the construction are
blocked, by the same mechanism.

---

## 2. The mechanism

Fix a good prime `ℓ ≠ 2`. The geometric deck group `G` acts on `J[2] = H¹(X, F₂)`, and
arithmetic Frobenius commutes with this action (it is `F₂[G]`-linear). Hence the arithmetic
Galois image is contained in the **centralizer**

    image ⊆ C_{Sp_{2g}(F₂)}(ρ(G)) = Aut_{F₂[G]}(J[2]) = (End_{F₂[G]}(J[2]))ˣ.

So `Gal(Q(J[2])/Q)` is a subquotient of the unit group of the endomorphism ring of `J[2]`
as an `F₂[G]`-module. If that unit group is solvable, `Q(J[2])` is solvable.

**Reduction to full 2-power torsion.** The image of `Gal(Q(J[2^∞])/Q)` in `GSp(T₂ J)`
surjects onto the mod-2 image with pro-2 kernel (the kernel of reduction mod 2). A pro-2
group is (pro-)solvable, and an extension of a solvable group by a pro-solvable group is
solvable. So **solvability of the mod-2 centralizer controls the entire 2-power torsion
field.** Computing `J[2]` suffices.

## 3. Lemma A — the algebraic engine (formally proved in Lean)

> **Lemma A.** Let `M` be a finite `F₂[G]`-module that is **indecomposable**. Then
> `Aut_{F₂[G]}(M)` is solvable.

*Proof.* Indecomposable + finite length ⟹ `E := End_{F₂[G]}(M)` is a **local** ring
(Fitting's lemma: the only idempotents are `0, 1`). `E` is finite, and a finite local ring
has prime-power order. Let `J = Ring.jacobson E` (two-sided; `E` is noncommutative in
general). Then:

- `E/J` is a finite **division ring**, hence a finite **field** by Wedderburn's little
  theorem; its unit group is cyclic, hence solvable.
- `J` is nilpotent (`E` is Artinian) and `x ↦ 1 + x` bijects `J` with `ker(Eˣ → (E/J)ˣ)`,
  so that kernel `1 + J` has `p`-power order — a `p`-group, hence solvable.
- `Eˣ` is an extension of the solvable `(E/J)ˣ` by the solvable `1 + J`, hence solvable. ∎

**Lean formalization:** `aristotle_solvable/FiniteLocalSolvable.lean` (287 lines, 15 lemmas,
**0 `sorry`/`axiom`/`admit`**, standard axiom profile). It proves, in full generality:

- `isSolvable_units_of_finite_local` : `[Ring E] [Finite E] [IsLocalRing E] → IsSolvable Eˣ`
- `isSolvable_moduleAut_of_localEnd` : finite module with local `End` ⟹ solvable `Autᵃ`
- `isLocalRing_end_of_indecomposable` : (all idempotents `0/1`) + `Nontrivial` ⟹ local `End`
  (the Fitting bridge)

The formalization was carried out by Aristotle (Harmonic). It corrected a genuine error in
the human skeleton, which had used the commutative-only `IsLocalRing.ResidueField`; the
application needs the noncommutative `Module.End`, so the proof was rebuilt around the
two-sided Jacobson radical and `littleWedderburn`. (Not independently recompiled outside
Aristotle; to re-verify run `lake build` + `#print axioms`.)

## 4. Lemma B — the geometric input (open; overwhelmingly supported)

> **Lemma B (conjecture).** For every 2-group Belyi map, `H¹(X, F₂)` is an indecomposable
> `F₂[G]`-module.

**Evidence:** exhaustive computation over all genus-`≥ 2` triples for `|G| ≤ 128`, the last
computationally feasible order (only 2-generated 2-groups occur, since the monodromy is a
quotient of `F₂`):

| `\|G\|` | genus-≥2 triples | indecomposable |
|---|---|---|
| 16 | 18 | 18 |
| 32 | 82 | 82 |
| 64 | 370 | 370 |
| 128 | 1538 | 1538 |
| **total** | **2008** | **2008 (100%)** |

Not a single decomposable `J[2]`, hence not a single nonsolvable centralizer, anywhere in
range. (Computation: `torsion_module.sage` for the module, MeatAxe
`MTX.Indecomposition`/`MTX.IsIndecomposable` for the multiplicity screen in
`torsion_shard.sage`; cross-checked on the hardest case `[32,5]` quo 1, genus 9.)

Lemma B is the sole remaining gap. Its proof strategy is the subject of the companion
document `writeup/lemma-B-open-problem.md`.

## 5. Both routes die together (Galois and non-Galois)

A *non-Galois* 2-group Belyi map is a subcover `X = X̃/H` of its Galois closure `X̃ → P¹`,
where `X̃` **is** a Galois 2-group Belyi map with deck group `G`. Then, up to isogeny,
`Jac(X) ∼ Jac(X̃)^H`, and `J(X)[2]` is — up to a small correction term — a **Galois-
equivariant subquotient** of `J(X̃)[2]`.

Since the arithmetic image on `J(X̃)[2]` is solvable (Lemma A + B applied to the Galois
closure), and **a subquotient of a solvable group's action is solvable**, the non-Galois
image is solvable too. The only "new" part of `J(X)[2]` not coming from `J(X̃)[2]` is the
Hochschild–Serre boundary `Hom(H, F₂)` (from the `H`-cover in characteristic 2), of
dimension equal to the number of generators of `H` (typically 2). This is far too small to
carry a nonsolvable symplectic representation such as `PSL₂(16) ⊂ Sp₈(F₂)` (which needs an
8-dimensional module). So the non-Galois route inherits solvability from its Galois closure.

**Consequence:** proving the Galois theorem (Lemma A + B) simultaneously kills the
non-Galois route. There is no independent hiding place for the prize.

*Technical points to finalize in a formal writeup:* the exact `F₂`-coefficient
Hochschild–Serre sequence for the (wildly ramified) `H`-cover `X̃ → X`, and the 2-power
isogeny discrepancy between `Jac(X)` and `Jac(X̃)^H`. Neither affects the "small boundary +
solvable subquotient" conclusion, but both deserve a clean statement.

## 6. Theorem (conditional on Lemma B)

> **Theorem.** For every 2-group Belyi map `φ: X → P¹` (Galois or non-Galois), the 2-power
> torsion field `Q(J[2^∞])` is a solvable extension of `Q`. In particular, **no 2-group
> Belyi map yields a nonsolvable number field ramified only at 2**, answering Q1.2.5 in the
> negative for the torsion field.

Unconditional content: Lemma A (Lean-verified) + the pro-2 reduction + the Galois-closure
argument reduce the whole statement to Lemma B. Lemma B holds for all `|G| ≤ 128`.

## 7. What is rigorous vs. what remains

| Component | Status |
|---|---|
| Frobenius `F₂[G]`-linear ⟹ image ⊆ centralizer | standard (Beckmann + Néron–Ogg–Shafarevich) |
| Indecomposable ⟹ solvable centralizer (Lemma A) | **proved, formalized in Lean** |
| Pro-2 reduction: `J[2]` controls `J[2^∞]` | standard |
| Non-Galois ⟹ Galois-closure subquotient | rigorous (boundary term needs clean statement) |
| `J[2]` indecomposable (Lemma B) | **open**; verified `\|G\| ≤ 128` (2008/2008) |

The one mathematical frontier is Lemma B. See the companion document.
